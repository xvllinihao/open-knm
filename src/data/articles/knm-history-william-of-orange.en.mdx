      <p>
        <strong>Willem van Oranje</strong> (1533–1584) was een hoge edelman in de
        Nederlanden. Hij stamde uit het huis <strong>Oranje-Nassau</strong> en werkte eerst
        voor koning <strong>Filips II</strong> van Spanje, die toen over de Nederlanden heerste.
      </p>
      <p>
        In de loop van de tijd werd hij een belangrijke stem tegen het harde bestuur en de

        <strong>godsdienstvrijheid</strong> moesten hebben en dat de gewesten meer

      </p>
      <p>
        Toen de spanningen opliepen, koos Willem de kant van de opstandelingen. Hij werd zo de{" "}

        <strong>"Vader des Vaderlands"</strong> genoemd.
      </p>
      <p>
        In 1584 werd hij in Delft vermoord. Toch bleef zijn naam belangrijk: de Nederlandse
        koning(in) komt nog steeds uit het huis <strong>Oranje-Nassau</strong>, en de nationale
        feestdag heet <strong>Koningsdag</strong>.
      </p>

      <h2>De Tachtigjarige Oorlog in het kort</h2>
      <p>
        De <strong>Tachtigjarige Oorlog (1568–1648)</strong> was een lange strijd tussen de
        Nederlanden en Spanje. Het begon als een opstand tegen hoge belastingen, religieuze
        vervolging en een koning die ver weg in Spanje woonde.
      </p>
      <p>
        In 1579 sloten verschillende noordelijke gewesten zich samen in de{" "}
        <strong>Unie van Utrecht (1579)</strong>. Dit verdrag legde de basis voor

      </p>
      <p>
        In 1581 volgde het <strong>Plakkaat van Verlatinghe (1581)</strong>. Daarin verklaarden

        <strong>niet langer hun wettige vorst</strong> was.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>Who was William of Orange?</h2>
      <p>
        <strong>William of Orange (Willem van Oranje)</strong> (1533–1584) was a high nobleman
        in the Low Countries from the <strong>House of Orange‑Nassau (Oranje-Nassau)</strong>.
        At that time the Netherlands were ruled by{" "}
        <strong>King Philip II of Spain (Filips II)</strong>, and William first worked on the
        king&apos;s side.
      </p>
      <p>
        Over time he strongly disagreed with the harsh rule and the persecution of{" "}
        <strong>Protestants (protestanten)</strong> in the region. He believed people should
        enjoy more <strong>freedom of religion (godsdienstvrijheid)</strong> and that the
        provinces (<strong>gewesten</strong>) should have more self‑rule.
      </p>
      <p>
        When tensions grew, William sided with the rebels and became the{" "}
        <strong>leader of the Revolt (leider van de Opstand)</strong> against Spain. Because of
        his role in the struggle for freedom and independence, he is often called the{" "}
        <strong>"Father of the Dutch nation" (Vader des Vaderlands)</strong>.
      </p>
      <p>
        In 1584 he was assassinated in Delft. His influence lives on: the Dutch royal family
        still belongs to the <strong>House of Orange‑Nassau</strong>, and the national holiday{" "}
        <strong>King&apos;s Day (Koningsdag)</strong> is closely connected with the colour
        orange and the House of Orange.
      </p>

      <h2>The Eighty Years&apos; War in brief</h2>
      <p>
        The <strong>Eighty Years&apos; War (Tachtigjarige Oorlog, 1568–1648)</strong> was a long
        conflict between the Dutch provinces and Spain. It started with dissatisfaction over{" "}
        <strong>high taxes</strong>, <strong>religious persecution</strong> and a king ruling
        from far away in Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
        In 1579 several northern provinces signed the{" "}
        <strong>Union of Utrecht (Unie van Utrecht, 1579)</strong>, agreeing to cooperate and
        defend each other. This treaty is often seen as the foundation of the later{" "}
        <strong>Dutch Republic (Republiek)</strong>.
      </p>
      <p>
        In 1581 they issued the{" "}
        <strong>Act of Abjuration (Plakkaat van Verlatinghe, 1581)</strong>, which declared that{" "}
        <strong>Philip II was no longer their legitimate ruler</strong>. It was like a{" "}
        &quot;divorce&quot; from the king, saying he had failed to protect his people.
      </p>
      <p>
        In 1648 the <strong>Treaty of Münster (Vrede van Münster, 1648)</strong> was signed.
        Spain officially recognised the{" "}
        <strong>
          Republic of the Seven United Netherlands (Republiek der Zeven Verenigde Nederlanden)
        </strong>{" "}
        as an <strong>independent state (onafhankelijke staat)</strong>, bringing the war to an
        end.
      </p>

      <h2>Why is this important for KNM?</h2>
      <p>
        In the <strong>KNM exam</strong> you usually do not have to memorise all dates, but you
        should understand key ideas: how the Netherlands became{" "}
        <strong>independent (onafhankelijk)</strong> from Spain, and why{" "}
        <strong>freedom of religion (godsdienstvrijheid)</strong> and{" "}
        <strong>tolerance (verdraagzaamheid)</strong> are important values in Dutch society.
      </p>
      <p>
        You can remember <strong>William of Orange</strong> as &quot;the man who led the
        Netherlands towards independence&quot; and the{" "}
        <strong>Eighty Years&apos; War</strong> as &quot;the long period in which the Dutch
        broke away from Spain and formed their own republic&quot;. This helps you understand
        the background when such topics appear in the KNM exam.
      </p>
